Claims


The invention claimed is:

1. A method comprising: filtering, using one or more processors, labeled measurements to determine conformance of the labeled measurements from at least some non-closed, non-contiguous regions of the labeled measurements; and generating, using the one or more processors, electrical signal sample values for display of a visualization of contiguous, closed regions of the filtered labeled measurements, the visualization depicting at least shape and size of the contiguous, closed regions, wherein the electrical signal sample values for display of the visualization are generated at a plurality of varying scale levels.

2. The method of claim 1, wherein the generating of the electrical signal sample values for display of the visualization includes extracting and/or adding features depicted by the visualization as a part of panning or zooming among the plurality of varying scale levels.

3. The method of claim 2, wherein the extracting and/or adding comprises forming boundaries of the contiguous, closed regions.

4. The method of claim 3, wherein, near the formed boundaries, an at least partial effect of at least one label comprises approaching a threshold value.

5. The method of claim 1, wherein the generating the electrical signal sample values for display includes omitting the labeled measurements at least partially in response to changing to a scale level at which the contiguous, closed regions are visualized.

6. The method of claim 5, wherein the changing comprises increasing scale level.

7. The method of claim 5, wherein the changing comprises decreasing scale level.

8. The method of claim 1, wherein contiguous, closed regions represent regions of the Earth's surface.

9. The method of claim 1, wherein at least one of the plurality of varying scale levels encompasses approximately the entire surface of the Earth.

10. The method of claim 1, wherein the labeled measurements are based at least in part on photographic images.

11. The method of claim 1, wherein the plurality of varying scale levels comprises a set of scale levels at approximately consistent scale intervals between a relatively low scale level and a relatively high scale level inclusive.

12. The method of claim 1, further comprising forming a single closed region from two or more regions at least partially in response to changing scale level.

13. The method of claim 1, further comprising forming two or more regions from a single closed region at least partially in response to changing scale level.

14. An apparatus, comprising: one or more processing units to: filter labeled measurements to determine conformance of the labeled measurements from at least some non-closed, non-contiguous regions of the labeled measurements; and employ one or more image processing techniques to the at least one non-closed, non-contiguous regions of the labeled measurements to depict at least shape and size of contiguous, closed regions of the labeled measurements, wherein the one or more image processing techniques is to be employed at a plurality of varying scale levels.

15. The apparatus of claim 14, wherein the one or more processing units is additionally to: extract and/or add features to be depicted by the one or more image processing techniques as a result of a zoom or a pan among the varying scale levels.

16. The apparatus of claim 15, wherein the one or more processing units is additionally to: form boundaries of the contiguous, closed regions, at least partially in response to the extraction and/or the addition of features.

17. The apparatus of claim 14, wherein the one or more processing units is additionally to: omit the labeled measurements at least partially in response to a change in scale level at which the contiguous, closed regions are to be visualized.

18. An article comprising: a non-transitory storage medium comprising instructions stored thereon which are executable by a special purpose computing apparatus to: filter labeled measurements to determine conformance of the labeled measurements from at least some detached regions of the labeled measurements; generate, from the at least some detached regions of the labeled measurements, a visualization of one or more contiguous, closed regions of the labeled measurements, the visualization to depict at least shape and size of the one or more contiguous, closed regions at a plurality of varying scale levels; and omit one or more labeled measurements at least partially in response to a change in scale level at which the one or more contiguous, closed regions are to be visualized.

19. The article of claim 18, wherein the non-transitory storage medium additionally comprises instructions stored thereon which are executable by the special purpose computing apparatus to: access a plurality of labeled captured images to represent the labeled measurements.

20. The article of claim 19, wherein the non-transitory storage medium additionally comprises instructions stored thereon which are executable by the special purpose computing apparatus to: form a single closed region from two or more regions at least partially in response to a change in scale level; and form two or more regions from a single closed region.

BACKGROUND

1. Field

This disclosure relates to processing labeled measurements to form a visual representation, at varying scales, of regions characterized by labels.

2. Information

In many applications, it may be useful to use computer-assisted graphics to display measurements in a manner that may be more easily interpreted by, for example, researchers, scientists, investigators, students, and/or others. For example, a medical researcher investigating spread of an illness or disease across a region, may find that computer-assisted images representing geographical distribution of the illness or disease may be a useful tool in determining locations at which disease-fighting resources may be positioned. Such positioning may facilitate, for example, more efficient and/or more effective deployment of healthcare assets in a manner that may reduce incidence of cases of affliction as well as increase an ability to treat those already afflicted.

In another example in which display of computer-assisted graphics may be useful, a social sciences researcher may study measurements of labels associated with photographs in a database in an effort to study regional and/or national trends. In one possible example, trends among certain age groups may be evaluated by studying types of photographs taken by individuals of certain age groups and/or locations at which photographic images are captured, for example. This research may be used, for example, to uncover factors contributing to trends so that, for example, ramifications of elections, political and/or economic decisions, etc., may be better understood. To facilitate investigation, computer-generated graphics, for example, may be used to assist a researcher in visualizing trends and/or assessing how trends may propagate within a society, region, nation, etc.

Unfortunately, contemporary computer visualization tool sets may not be capable of assisting in epidemiological and/or social sciences investigations, for example. Thus, various types of epidemiology and/or social science research may be hindered by an inability to comprehensively and/or accurately visualize various phenomena. Researchers, for example, may be constrained to using less-effective and/or manual tools, which may be slow and/or cumbersome to manipulate. Further, communication of results of investigations to an audience, perhaps by way of computer-assisted imagery, may be less effective or even impaired. This may potentially reduce the benefit of conducting the investigation.

At a particular level, which may represent a relatively high-resolution representation of a portion of the surface of the Earth, a computing device may make use of a first two-dimensional histogram to form iso-contours of a region, for example. At a particular level, labeled measurements used to construct a two-dimensional histogram may be filtered to determine a particular label's prominence and/or conformance relative to other labels, for example. Within a region, a label's prominence and/or conformance may meet or exceed a threshold at least approximately. In response to a user panning to visualize a different portion of the surface of the Earth, or perhaps in response to a user selecting to visualize a similar portion of the surface of the Earth at a different resolution (e.g., high zoom level, low zoom level), a computing device may generate a second two-dimensional histogram. A second two-dimensional histogram, as well as additional histograms, may, for example, permit a user to visualize a region at approximately consistent scale levels, such as relatively high scale levels (e.g., low zoom), relatively low scale levels (e.g., high zoom) and/or at varying scale levels in between.

In an implementation, a representation of at least one identified region encompassing locations of measured labels may be exhibited using one or more "layers" on a display. It is noted that the term layer may be associated with a particular scale level out of a variety of possible scale levels. At a given layer, which may represent, for example, a relatively high-resolution representation of a portion of the surface of the earth, a region may be identified by detecting one or more outermost iso-contours that may bound an area within which prominence and/or conformance of label measurements relative to other label measurements may drop below a threshold level, for example. In this context, the term prominence refers to a characteristic in which some label measurements may tend to stand out among a larger set of label measurements. In some implementations, a weighting function that may be applied to labeled measurements to enhance a contribution (e.g. prominence) of some labeled measurements in comparison other labeled measurements Likewise, in this context, the term conformance refers to a characteristic in which some label measurements may tend to be consistent within a larger set of label measurements. To display an identified region covering a larger area, for example, representing a portion of the surface of the Earth at somewhat lower resolution, e.g., another layer or different scale level, a computing device may generate a second histogram. A second histogram may, at least in part, permit a user to increase or to decrease a zoom level in a reasonably consistent manner between a lower resolution layer (e.g., relatively higher scale level) encompassing a relatively large identified region and a higher resolution layer (e.g., relatively lower scale level) encompassing a smaller identified region, for example, and levels between as well.

In an implementation, a second layer may be generated using a smoothing process, such as with respect to a previously constructed histogram.
